On Thursday, Hong Kong's Legislative Council <a data-analytics-module="summary_bullets" href="https://www.businessinsider.com/hong-kong-passes-china-national-anthem-law-explainer-2020-6">criminalized the mocking of the Chinese national anthem</a>.</li><li>March organizer Lee Cheuk-yan said: "Next year will be even more dangerous. Those convicted face a maximum three years in jail and a 50,000 Hong Kong dollar ($6,450) fine. </p><p>The first line of the anthem — The March of the Volunteers — states "arise, ye who refuse to be slaves," and urges people to help China to prosper.</p><p><figure data-e2e-name="image-figure-image" data-media-container="image" data-type="img"><p><img alt="A worker takes a photo after helping to display a banner reminding people against group gatherings in a barricaded area of Victoria Park in Hong Kong on June 4, 2020, after the annual vigil which traditionally takes place in the park to mark the 1989 Tiananmen Square crackdown was banned on public health grounds because of the COVID-19 coronavirus pandemic. <h2>The national security bill explained</h2><p>The national security bill passed last month will see Hong Kong effectively lose many powers given to it as a Special Administrative Region of China.</p><p>Since the 1997 handover from the British, Beijing has allowed Hong Kong to operate under a "one country, two systems" policy for many years. Neighboring city Macau, a former Portuguese colony, also had that system in place.</p><p>That understanding looks to be coming to an end.</p><p>Under the new law, China can <a data-analytics-module="body_link" data-analytics-post-depth="20" href="https://www.businessinsider.com/china-hong-kong-national-security-law-how-it-could-work-2020-5">draft and propose national security laws</a>, impose its own police force in the city, suppress activists and protests, and lay down <a data-analytics-module="body_link" data-analytics-post-depth="20" href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/05/28/world/asia/china-hong-kong-crackdown.html">long jail sentences</a> for those in opposition.</p>



<p>Even before the new law was enacted, China sought to stamp out the city's monthslong, pro-democracy demonstrations that began in June 2019.</p><p><figure data-e2e-name="image-figure-image" data-media-container="image" data-type="img"><p><img alt="Xi Jinping" data-content-type="image/jpeg" data-srcs="{"https://i.insider.com/5ed8c50daee6a8450012be88":{"contentType":"image/jpeg","aspectRatioW":2,"aspectRatioH":1}}" src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg' viewBox='0 0 1 1'%3E%3C/svg%3E"></img>
